Ep.4 德语时刻 | Jojo 与 Yoyo

中文稿

【开场】
Hallo, hier ist Jojo's Pod. 你好，这里是 Yoyo's Pod.

【字母 J 的发音旅行】
欸，不是 Jojo 吗，怎么变成了 Yoyo。嗯，在德语里 Jojo 就会读作 Yoyo。

我曾经一直以为，字母 J 在人名里只有一个发音 ([d͡ʒ])，比如，John (约翰)、Jack (杰克)。后来我去西班牙时，才发现原来 J 还有其他发音 ([h])，比如，Juan (胡安)、José (何塞)。而仔细看法语里的人名，会发现英语里的 Jean 读作 [ʒɑ̃] (冉)，Jean Valjean [ʒɑ̃ val.ʒɑ̃] (冉阿让)。现在又知道，原来在德语里 J 是 [jɔt]，Johannes。

【字母 J 的历史来源】
而其实 Johannes、Jean、Juan、John 这些名字，都可以追溯到一路共同来源，古希腊语的 Ioannes，更久远还可以到希伯来语的 Yohanan。它的意思是，"Jehovah has favored"，耶和华眷顾。神力无边，因此不难想到这个名字还有数个变体，Johann，Hans，Jan.....

这里你会发现，无论是古希腊还是希伯来语的字母表里都没有 J ，只有 I。也就是，在古希腊字母表里只有 ι (iota)；因此，古拉丁文里也没有字母 J，它其实就是 I 的变体，到了中世纪，J 才逐渐从 I 独立出来。

【电影《乔乔的异想世界》里的 Jojo】
说回到 Jojo。我特意去查了 Jojo Rabbit 《乔乔的异想世界》那部电影，看看里面是怎样发音的；果然是 Jojo。虽然故事在德国，但毕竟是英语国家拍的。不过，小男孩本名 Johannes，在德语里他的小名一定是 Jojo，悠悠，听起来就好像是在说悠悠球。对啦，Jojo 在德语里的意思正是悠悠球。这让我想起来，一个奇怪的人，给了我两个悠悠球，我怀疑他是故意的。

【片头曲 甲壳虫乐队 I Want to Hold Your Hand 的德语版本】
说回这部电影，对德语有些了解后再看，发现不少之前没有注意到的细节。比如，影片开头 The Beatles 那首旋律为大家熟知的 I Want to Hold Your Hand，竟然是原唱德语版本：Komm, gib mir deine Hand。而搭配 Jojo 在个人崇拜中亢奋暴走的，刚好是那句 Du nimmst mir den Verstand /You take away my reason from me 你夺去了我的理智，或者，You drive me crazy 你让我疯狂。这一安排，让感觉瞬间丰富起来，讽刺又搞笑。并且，其实英语版本里并没有这一句，对应的是 I think you'll understand 我想你会明白；这就更增加了一层张力。不知道甲壳虫乐队看到自己的歌被用在这里会是什么感想 :)

【片尾曲 大卫·鲍伊 The Heroes 的德语版本】
还有就是片尾 David Bowie 大卫·鲍伊 The Heroes，居然也是原唱的德语版本，Helden。结合这首歌的创作背景，似乎站在墙边的德语版更是有着切肤之痛。半个世纪之后的今天，有形的无形的墙似乎并未减少，而因之形成的各种对立也貌似更加激烈。这真的无解吗？

【语言拓展理解力】
我们知道，感受拓宽感受的边界，认知拓宽认知的边界；而跟随不同语言的交错，可以编织出更深刻更广阔的理解之地，也才有可能去探寻更深远的惊奇与未知，而创造力也在其中成形。学习不同语言，无疑是开拓的利器。尤其对于那个成长在确信自己掌握着绝对正确的大环境之下的 Jojo。否则，他在自己的进行曲中唱到的 Für das Recht und die Unschuld /for justice and innocence 为了正义与纯真，也只会变成他自己眼前的罪恶。

【尝试不同语言是个体摆脱自我牢笼的可能方式之一】
爱因斯坦在《我的世界观》里写过一句话：一个人真正的价值首先取决于他在什么程度上和在什么意义上从自我解放出来。而我想，多样的语言，大概正是从自我中伸展出去的一种方式。

不同语言之间的对比与各种脉络关联，让简单的概念变得丰富。比如，Jojo 与 Yoyo。我有时会想，如果汉族也在地中海边上，会发展出什么样的一种语言；但那样就未必会有“相见时难别亦难，东风无力百花残”的那一种婉转伤感；也不会有“君不见黄河之水天上来”的那一种磅礴豪迈。所以，我想我还是会选择，跟随方块字去探寻字母的世界，再跟随字母的世界返回到方块字的迷人。There And Back Again. Forever and Ever.

【里尔克诗歌】
最后，我想以电影 Jojo Rabbit 片尾引用的里尔克 (Rainer Maria Rilke) 诗句来完成今天这一期节目：

Lass dir Alles geschehn: / Let everything happen to you 让一切发生在你身上：
Schönheit und Schrecken. / Beauty and terror 美与惊惧。
Man muss nur gehn: / Just keep going 只要继续前行：
Kein Gefühl ist das fernste. / No feeling is final 没有哪种感受会是终点。

我还想加上原诗的后面两句：
Nah ist das Land, / Near is the land，那块境地就在近旁
das sie das Leben nennen. / that they call life 他们称之为“生命”。
Du wirst es erkennen / You will know it 你将会认出它
an seinem Ernste. / by its gravity 因它的深重。

【生命因其深重而飞升】
我想少年 Jojo 们可以勇敢地去探索，Beauty and terror，无需畏惧。而中年开始 Jojo 们或许会多一层体会，欢愉与悲伤，爱与痛，相互交织。生命因其深重而被感知。

【结尾】
今天就到这里。谢谢你的收听。Χαίρετε (希腊语 再见) Miao~

English Transcript

[Opening]
Hallo, hier ist Jojo's Pod. Hello, this is Yoyo’s Pod.

[The Pronunciation Journey of the Letter J]
Wait, isn’t it Jojo? How did it become Yoyo? Well, in German, “Jojo” is pronounced as “Yoyo.”

For a long time, I thought the letter J in names always had the same sound [d͡ʒ], as in John or Jack. Later, when I went to Spain, I discovered that J could also sound like [h], as in Juan or José. In French names, however, J takes yet another form [ʒ], as in Jean. Remember? Jean Valjean [ʒɑ̃ val.ʒɑ̃]. And now, I’ve learned that in German, J is pronounced [jɔt], as in Johannes.

[The Historical Origins of the Letter J]
In fact, Johannes, Jean, Juan, and John all trace back to a common source: the Greek Ioannes, and even further to the Hebrew Yohanan. It means “Jehovah has favored.” With such divine significance, it is no surprise that this name has countless variants: Johann, Hans, Jan...

Interestingly, neither the Greek nor the Hebrew alphabets originally had the letter J—only I. In Greek it appears as ι (iota). Consequently, classical Latin also lacked J. The letter J was in fact a variation of I, and only in the Middle Ages did it gradually emerge as a distinct character.

[Jojo in the Film Jojo Rabbit]
Let’s return to Jojo. I looked up the film Jojo Rabbit to check how the name was pronounced—and indeed, it is Jojo. Although the story is set in Germany, the film was produced in an English-speaking country. The boy’s real name is Johannes, and in German his nickname would naturally be Jojo—which also happens to mean “yo-yo.” That reminded me of the strange person who gave me two yo-yos, one and later another.

[The German Version of the Beatles’ Song I Want to Hold Your Hand]
Watching the film again with some knowledge of German, I noticed details I had previously missed. For instance, the opening song, the Beatles’ I Want to Hold Your Hand, is actually the German version Komm, gib mir deine Hand. Paired with Jojo’s frenzied devotion to a cult of personality, it coincides with the lyric Du nimmst mir den Verstand (You take away my reason from me, or You drive me crazy). This creates a striking mix of irony and comedy. Interestingly, this line does not appear in the English version, which instead has I think you’ll understand. The difference adds yet another layer of tension. Can you imagine what the Beatles themselves would’ve thought, seeing their song used like that?

[The German Version of David Bowie’s Heroes]
At the film’s ending, it’s David Bowie—Heroes—but in German: Helden. In light of the song’s historical background, the German rendition—sung at the wall—carries a visceral weight. And honestly, fifty years later, walls haven’t really gone away—some are concrete, some are invisible—and the divisions they create? If anything, they’re sharper now. Is there really no way out?

[Expanding Understanding Through Languages]
Here’s the thing: feelings push the edge of feelings, knowledge pushes the edge of knowledge. Following the crossings of different languages, one can weave a broader and deeper ground of understanding, and from there reach into wonder and the unknown, where creativity also takes shape. Learning new languages really is like unlocking a whole new toolbox. Especially for someone like Jojo, who grew up convinced that he already possessed the one absolute truth. Without stepping beyond that, his marching song Für das Recht und die Unschuld (“for justice and innocence”) would only serve the very evil right in front of him.

[Learning Languages as a Way Beyond the Self]
Einstein once wrote in The World as I See It: “The true value of a human being is determined primarily by the measure and the sense in which he has attained to liberation from the self.” I believe that engaging with multiple languages can be one of the ways to stretch beyond the confines of the self.

Comparing and crossing languages enriches even the simplest concepts. Take Jojo and Yoyo as an example. I sometimes wonder: if the Han people had lived by the Mediterranean, what kind of language would have emerged? But then we would likely not have the sorrowful delicacy of lines like, “Partings are hard, the east wind is weak, and the blossoms are fading”; nor the heroic grandeur of “Do you not see the Yellow River rushing down from heaven?” That is why I choose to follow Chinese characters into the world of letters, and then return from letters back into the fascination of characters. There And Back Again. Forever and Ever.

[Rilke’s Poem]
Finally, I wish to close today’s episode with the lines of Rainer Maria Rilke (1875–1926), quoted at the end of Jojo Rabbit:

Lass dir Alles geschehn: / Let everything happen to you.
Schönheit und Schrecken. / Beauty and terror.
Man muss nur gehn: / Just keep going.
Kein Gefühl ist das fernste. / No feeling is final.

And I would add the two lines that follow in the original:
Nah ist das Land, / Near is the land,
das sie das Leben nennen. / that they call life.
Du wirst es erkennen / You will know it
an seinem Ernste. / by its gravity.

[Life’s Gravity]
I imagine the young Jojos can step forward bravely, embracing both beauty and terror without fear. And as Jojos grow older, perhaps they will come to feel more deeply how joy and sorrow, love and pain, intertwine. Life is recognized precisely through its gravity.

[Closing]
That’s all for today. Thank you for listening. Χαίρετε (Greek for “goodbye”). Miao~
