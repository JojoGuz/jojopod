00:15【刻 / Carving】

最近重新开始做印品，先用一小块木版做实验。不出意外的是，各种意外都会出现；尤其是刻完后怎么都找不到那一管油墨，它难道长腿跑了吗？找不到东西时的沮丧，有时会被重新发现一些东西的喜悦覆盖，比如，做橡皮章的印盒被我翻来翻去翻出来了，还有一些橡皮块，索性随手刻了一个。

刻刀在光滑的又弹又韧的橡皮块上划过的感觉，与在木板上柔软的面层与粗粝的纤维间划过的感觉完全不同：在橡皮上丝滑般流动如同巧克力，却也可能是甜蜜的陷阱；木板以其坚韧对抗，带来某种实在感，也将自身融入到另一个生命进程。

但，哪一种刻都会手酸。

Lately I’ve started making prints again, experimenting first with a small woodblock. And, as expected, all sorts of unexpected things happened — especially when, after carving, I somehow couldn’t find that tube of ink. Had it grown legs and run away? The frustration of losing something was, however, soon covered by the joy of rediscovery something else. While searching, I dug out my old stamp pad and a few rubber blocks. so I carved a little stamp on a whim.

The feeling of the knife sliding through the smooth, bouncy rubber is completely different from cutting into the soft surface and coarse fibers of wood: on rubber it flows like chocolate — a silky sweetness that can easily become a trap; on wood, its resistance gives a sense of reality, as if it merges itself into another life process.

Either way, your hand aches from carving.

01:21【埃舍尔：手与神性 / Escher: The Hand and Divinity】

看来，埃舍尔的话还是很有道理的。前几天翻出那本从埃舍尔博物馆背回来的厚重大书——《埃舍尔的魔法》(The Magic of M.C.Escher)；数以年计这本书在书架上静立吃土，在重读中我才发现，埃舍尔在信件与讲座里，说过这么多富有意味的话语。比如关于“手艺”，他是这样讲的：

在创造任何图像的过程中，手是一种奇迹般精妙的工具，它是精神与物质之间的媒介。但插画家并不了解我们版画艺术家所说的“手艺”—— 那种与难以驾驭的材料搏斗、去征服物质的敌意的过程。而木刻家与雕刻家对此却心知肚明—— 有点像木匠与木料相处时的那种知觉。我为插画家感到遗憾，因为他被剥夺了一种极大的喜悦。[1]

所以你看，between spirit and matter, struggle, conquering, resistance....，埃舍尔将那种在物质世界中无疑被视作是苦力，并且时而会产生挫败感的手的劳作，转变为通往精神世界的阶梯，而在这一过程中几乎必然出现的专注，也成为接近神性的时间流动。

Apparently, Escher was right. A few days ago, I reopened the heavy book I once carried back from the Escher Museum — _The Magic of M.C. Escher_. It had been standing quietly on the shelf for years, gathering dust. Only in rereading did I realize how many meaningful things Escher had said in his letters and lectures. For example, about craftsmanship, he wrote:

As far as the craft is concerned, I know of course that for the creation of any image whatsoever the hand is the miraculously refined tool, the intermediary between spirit and matter. But the illustrator doesn't know anything about what we graphic artists call "the craft" — the struggle with the unmanageable material, the process of conquering a hostile material resistance, which the wood-carver and the engraver do know (more or less in the way the carpenter knows it), and I feel sorry for the illustrator because he is thus deprived of a great joy.

Between spirit and matter — struggle, conquering, resistance… Escher turned what might otherwise be seen as mere manual labor, often accompanied by frustration, into a path toward the spiritual world. The focus that comes with carving, in itself, a flow of time approaching the divine.

02:56【埃舍尔：美与秩序 / Escher: Beauty and Order】

埃舍尔所思考的神性当然不是宗教里的神，在他看来，神性与日常，对应的是，秩序与混乱；他这样讲到：

如果你问我："你为什么要做这些傻事——这些完全客观、没有任何个人色彩的事？" 那我只能回答："我停不下来。" 这个问题，从1500年到1600年间，那些人——丢勒、帕乔利、巴巴罗，甚至达·芬奇——也没有得到令人满意的答案。无疑，他们最初和我一样，是出于对纯粹形式的兴趣：规则形体的美与秩序是压倒性的。它们在那里，你无能为力。若你坚持要谈“神”，那它们确实有某种神性——至少，不是人性的。[2]

这里的 the beauty and the order，美与秩序，是埃舍尔一直在尝试捕捉并期待呈现出来的，在平面里暗示空间，在静态里暗示动感，他持续增加复杂性，在逻辑里构建秩序感。有趣的是，即使他故意调侃秩序，构建一些不合逻辑的悖论，却依然不会因非秩序而成为混乱，比如他的作品《不可能的楼梯》或《手画手》。这里我会想到《集异壁》那本书里的自我指涉，以及列维纳斯《时间与他者》里的他者才让时间真实存在，究竟是我还是他，我怕是又要把自己先说晕了，这一线索就此打住。

说回到秩序，似乎总是冰冷的，属于机械的，在埃舍尔那里是神性的召唤，不属于人类；然而，在薛定谔那里，秩序却属于人类，并且生命体恰好有着高层次的秩序。

The divinity Escher contemplated was, of course, not a religious one. For him, divinity corresponded to the relationship between order and chaos in everyday life. He once said:

If you were to ask, why do you do such silly things, such absolute objective things that have nothing personal any more? Then all I can answer is: I cannot stop it. This particular case has never been satisfactorily resolved, as far as I know, by that group of folks, around 1500 and 1600, Dürer, Pacioli, Barbaro, and even Leonardo. Undoubtedly they were originally interested in pure form in the same way as I am: the beauty and the order of regular bodies are overwhelming. There's nothing you can do, for they're there. If you then insist on speaking about god: they have something divine, at least nothing human.

"The beauty and the order" — this was what Escher constantly sought to capture and reveal: suggesting spatial in the flat, dynamic in the static, building complexity through logic. Interestingly, even when he playfully mocked order by constructing impossible paradoxes, as in _Ascending and Descending_ or _Drawing Hands_, his images never fell into chaos.

This reminds me of the self-reference in _Gödel, Escher, Bach_, and of Levinas’s idea in _Time and the Other_ — that the Other makes time real. But before I confuse myself again, let’s leave that thread for another day.

Back to order: it often seems cold, mechanical. Yet for Escher, it was a call of divinity, something beyond the human. For Schrödinger, however, order belongs precisely to the living — it is the essential structure of life itself.

05:10【薛定谔：秩序与生命 / Schrödinger: Order and Life】

在薛定谔的《生命是什么》，这本我历时三年才读完的经典小书里，有这样一段话：

一个活的有机体，似乎是一个宏观体系，它的某些行为部分地接近于那种纯粹的机械行为 (与热力学行为相对)，而这种机械行为正是当温度趋近绝对零度、分子无序被消除时，一切体系所趋向的状态。[3]

可以看到，物理学家的角度是很有趣的；通常我们会觉得生物行为与机械行为不可相提并论，生命是自由的难以预测的，而在他们眼中，我们这些生命体其实比机械还机械，或者说我们才是有绝对秩序的纯粹机械；当然我们的秩序"齿轮"，"非周期性晶体"，是来自于"上帝的量子力学有史以来最精致的杰作"。

In Schrödinger’s _What Is Life?_ — this little book that somehow took me three years to finish — there’s a line that says:

The living organism seems to be a macroscopic system which in part of its behaviour approaches to that purely mechanical (as contrasted with thermodynamical) conduct to which all systems tend, as the temperature approaches the absolute zero and the molecular disorder is removed.

It’s a fascinating perspective from a physicist. Normally, we would think biological behavior and mechanical behavior have nothing in common — that life is free, unpredictable. But in Schrödinger’s view, we living beings are, in a sense, _more mechanical than machines themselves_. We are the purest mechanisms of order. Our gears — our "aperiodic crystals" — are, as he wrote, "the finest masterpiece ever achieved along the lines of the Lord's quantum mechanic."

06:14【艺术与负熵 / Art and Negative Entropy】

经常有人用一句话概括薛定谔这一本书，生命以负熵为食；虽然远远不足以体现这本书的精彩，但至少让人们普遍知道：

生物体维持自身稳定在相当高的有序性水平（=相当低的熵水平）的机制，实际上在于不断地从其环境中吸取有序性。[4]

我很自然地会想到，这种有序性除了物质类的有秩序的食物，是否也一定会有精神类的有秩序的形式。比如，音乐里听觉上的音高排列，绘画里视觉上的形式构成，舞蹈里肢体的节奏比例，文学里的叙事结构，还有生活中有着优良设计的物品，比如，一只优雅的杯子、一张恰到好处的纸....... 所有这些作为秩序，也在为维持人体的有序性水平提供能量，尽管它们难以计量。

因此艺术也许就是另一种负熵。艺术作者从看起来混乱无序的世界中，感受和提取形式的秩序，再以创造将其释放。

其实，我一直觉得老大师们之所以能画出杰作，并非因为他们完全模仿绘画对象，仅仅是模仿的像，而是他们在意识中，或潜意识中，掌握了某种可以言说，或无法言说的——秩序。

People often summarize Schrödinger’s book with a single line: _“Life feeds on negative entropy.”_This phrase doesn’t capture the depth of the book, but it does reveal a key idea:

Thus the device by which an organism maintains itself stationary at a fairly high level of orderliness ( = fairly low level of entropy) really consists in continually sucking orderliness from its environment.

Naturally, I can’t help but wonder — this “orderliness” must include not only physical food, but also _spiritual_ forms of order. For instance, the arrangement of tones in music, the compositional balance in painting, the rhythmic proportions of dance, the narrative structure in literature — even the well-designed objects in daily life: an elegant cup, a perfectly textured sheet of paper...All these forms of order also nourish us. They provide energy to maintain our own internal orderliness, even if that energy cannot be measured.

So perhaps _art itself_ is another kind of negative entropy. That artists discover and absorb order from a seemingly chaotic world, and through creation, give it back to the world.

I’ve always felt that the great old masters created masterpieces not because they merely imitated appearances, but because, consciously or unconsciously, they grasped something deeper — speakable or unspeakable sense of _order_.

07:51【印 / The Print】

说回到我做的实验印品，一块小木版和一个橡皮章，无论刻的怎样粗糙，印的时候总会有种特别的小兴奋，彷佛期待着某种不可预见，而在种种的不可预见里，我们才有可能与神性相遇。

Coming back to my small print experiments — a tiny woodblock, a rubber stamp — no matter how rough the carving, there’s always a quiet excitement when I make a print, as if awaiting something unpredictable. And it’s within these unpredictable moments that we might, perhaps, encounter the divine.
